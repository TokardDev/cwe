# CWE-1297 - Unprotected Confidential Information on Device is Accessible by OSAT Vendors

## CWE Explanation

This weakness arises when a hardware product exposes sensitive data to Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test (OSAT) vendors during the manufacturing process. Chipmakers often outsource fabrication, assembly, and testing to third-party facilities. During these stages, devices are typically in a pre-production state where debug interfaces and test modes are fully open, and confidential data (like keys or proprietary algorithms) might be present in Non-Volatile Memory (NVM) or other storage.

If this confidential information is not minimized, encrypted, or scrubbed before the device is sent to the OSAT, it becomes vulnerable. The OSAT environment involves multiple risks, including accidental leaks, compromised IT systems, or malicious insiders. Unlike a fully vertically integrated manufacturing process, the chipmaker has less control over the security of the OSAT facility, making any exposed data a significant liability.

The root cause is a failure to enforce a "need-to-know" principle for the manufacturing chain, where the device retains more sensitive information than is strictly necessary for the OSAT's role, or fails to protect that information from being read during testing and assembly.

## Impact Assessment

| Aspect | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Confidentiality** | The attacker can extract confidential data such as global decryption keys or trade secrets. |
| **Integrity** | Low impact (*Integrity might be compromised if the attacker uses the extracted secrets to sign malicious firmware, but the primary impact is confidentiality loss*). |
| **Availability** | Low impact (*Availability loss is not the direct result, though compromised keys could lead to denial of service attacks later*). |

## Attack Scenario

An attacker (a malicious insider at the OSAT facility or an external actor who has compromised the OSAT's network) is able to physically interact with pre-production devices or access their test data. In the next step, the attacker reads the contents of the device's Non-Volatile Memory (NVM) via open test interfaces or debug ports. Due to insufficient scrubbing or protection mechanisms, the NVM contains a "master" unlock key intended for internal use only. The attacker successfully extracts this key, which allows them to bypass security protections on all shipped devices of that product generation.

## MITRE Reference Data

### Description

The product does not adequately protect confidential information on the device from being accessed by Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test (OSAT) vendors.

### Extended Description

In contrast to complete vertical integration of architecting, designing, manufacturing, assembling, and testing chips all within a single organization, an organization can choose to simply architect and design a chip before outsourcing the rest of the process to OSAT entities (e.g., external foundries and test houses). In the latter example, the device enters an OSAT facility in a much more vulnerable pre-production stage where many debug and test modes are accessible. Therefore, the chipmaker must place a certain level of trust with the OSAT. To counter this, the chipmaker often requires the OSAT partner to enter into restrictive non-disclosure agreements (NDAs). Nonetheless, OSAT vendors likely have many customers, which increases the risk of accidental sharing of information. There may also be a security vulnerability in the information technology (IT) system of the OSAT facility. Alternatively, a malicious insider at the OSAT facility may carry out an insider attack. Considering these factors, it behooves the chipmaker to minimize any confidential information in the device that may be accessible to the OSAT vendor.

Logic errors during design or synthesis could misconfigure the interconnection of the debug components, which could provide improper authorization to sensitive information.

### Demonstrative Examples

Example 1

The following example shows how an attacker can take advantage of a piece of confidential information that has not been protected from the OSAT.

Suppose the preproduction device contains NVM (a storage medium that by definition/design can retain its data without power), and this NVM contains a key that can unlock all the parts for that generation. An OSAT facility accidentally leaks the key.

Compromising a key that can unlock all the parts of a generation can be devastating to a chipmaker.

The likelihood of such a compromise can be reduced by ensuring all memories on the preproduction device are properly scrubbed.

## Remediations from MITRE

| Phase | Strategy | Mitigation | Effectiveness |
|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| Architecture and Design | — | Ensure that when an OSAT vendor is allowed to access test interfaces necessary for preproduction and returned parts, the vendor only pulls the minimal information necessary. Also, architect the product in such a way that, when an "unlock device" request comes, it only unlocks that specific part and not all the parts for that product line. Ensure that the product's non-volatile memory (NVM) is scrubbed of all confidential information and secrets before handing it over to an OSAT. Arrange to secure all communication between an OSAT facility and the chipmaker. | Moderate |

*Source: MITRE CWE Database*

## Recommended Remediations

| Phase | Remediation | Priority |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Architecture and Design | Design a pre-OSAT scrubbing mechanism that automatically erases all confidential data (keys, proprietary firmware, configuration secrets) from NVM before the device leaves the chipmaker's facility, with verification that the scrub completed successfully. | Critical |
| Architecture and Design | Architect per-device unlock credentials so that compromising one device's test access does not grant access to other devices in the same product line. Use device-unique keys derived from PUFs or per-device fused secrets. | Critical |
| Integration | Restrict OSAT test interfaces to expose only the minimum data necessary for assembly and test operations. Implement hardware-enforced access tiers so that OSAT tools cannot read security-sensitive registers or memory regions. | High |
| Policy | Require all OSAT communication channels (data transfers, test results, device images) to be encrypted end-to-end, and mandate regular security audits of the OSAT facility's IT infrastructure and physical access controls. | High |

*These remediations are recommended best practices and are not sourced from MITRE.*
