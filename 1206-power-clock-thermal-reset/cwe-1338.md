## CWE Explanation

This weakness arises when a hardware device lacks sufficient safeguards to monitor and manage its operating temperature. All electronic circuits dissipate energy as heat, particularly during intensive operations. In semiconductor devices, this effect is amplified by higher operating frequencies and leakage currents, which can create a positive feedback loop leading to thermal runaway.

If a system does not include necessary protections—such as on-die thermal sensors, platform-level cooling mechanisms (like fans), or logic to throttle performance—it becomes vulnerable to software-induced overheating. An attacker can exploit this deficiency by executing specific code sequences designed to maximize power consumption, effectively turning the software into a "power virus."

The absence of thermal limits means that such malicious workloads can drive the device's temperature beyond safe operating margins. This can result in immediate system instability or shutdown (Denial of Service) and, in severe cases, cause irreversible physical damage to the hardware components (Permanent Denial of Service).

## Attack Scenario

An attacker is able to execute arbitrary code on a processor core within the target system. The attacker deploys a "power virus"—a program specifically crafted to execute a sequence of instructions that maximizes the processor's power consumption and heat generation. Due to the absence of hardware thermal sensors or an active cooling management system (such as dynamic frequency scaling or fan control), the system fails to detect the rising temperature or mitigate the thermal stress. Consequently, the device overheats, leading to a thermal shutdown or permanent physical damage to the silicon, resulting in a complete loss of availability.

## MITRE Reference Data

### Description

A hardware device is missing or has inadequate protection features to prevent overheating.

### Extended Description

Hardware, electrical circuits, and semiconductor silicon have thermal side effects, such that some of the energy consumed by the device gets dissipated as heat and increases the temperature of the device. For example, in semiconductors, higher-operating frequency of silicon results in higher power dissipation and heat. The leakage current in CMOS circuits increases with temperature, and this creates positive feedback that can result in thermal runaway and damage the device permanently.

Any device lacking protections such as thermal sensors, adequate platform cooling, or thermal insulation is susceptible to attacks by malicious software that might deliberately operate the device in modes that result in overheating. This can be used as an effective denial of service (DoS) or permanent denial of service (PDoS) attack.

Depending on the type of hardware device and its expected usage, such thermal overheating can also cause safety hazards and reliability issues. Note that battery failures can also cause device overheating but the mitigations and examples included in this submission cannot reliably protect against a battery failure.

There can be similar weaknesses with lack of protection from attacks based on overvoltage or overcurrent conditions. However, thermal heat is generated by hardware operation and the device should implement protection from overheating.

### Demonstrative Examples

Example 1

Malicious software running on a core can execute instructions that consume maximum power or increase core frequency. Such a power-virus program could execute on the platform for an extended time to overheat the device, resulting in permanent damage.

Execution core and platform do not support thermal sensors, performance throttling, or platform-cooling countermeasures to ensure that any software executing on the system cannot cause overheating past the maximum allowable temperature.

The platform and SoC should have failsafe thermal limits that are enforced by thermal sensors that trigger critical temperature alerts when high temperature is detected. Upon detection of high temperatures, the platform should trigger cooling or shutdown automatically.

## Recommended Remediations

| Phase | Remediation | Priority |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Architecture and Design | Integrate on-die thermal sensors with hardware-enforced shutdown thresholds that cannot be overridden or disabled by software. | Critical |
| Architecture and Design | Implement hardware-controlled dynamic frequency and voltage scaling (DVFS) that automatically throttles performance when thermal thresholds are approached, independent of software control. | Critical |
| Implementation | Design platform cooling systems (fans, heat sinks) with closed-loop thermal management controlled by hardware logic, not solely by OS-level software. | High |
| Implementation | Add thermal monitoring redundancy: use multiple independent thermal sensors so that a single sensor failure or tampering does not blind the system to overheating. | High |
| Testing | Perform thermal stress testing with worst-case software workloads (power viruses) to validate that hardware thermal protections activate correctly and prevent damage. | Medium |

*These remediations are recommended best practices and are not sourced from MITRE.*
